What Changed in This Version
✓ RF = DF. One metric, one name. "Release Frequency" and "Deployment Frequency" are the same thing for your service line. Unified as "Release Frequency (RF)" throughout. No distinction needed.
✓ DataSight is the primary metric source for all DORA metrics. RF, LTDD, CFR, MTTR, automated CRs — all come from DataSight automatically. No GitHub API needed for these. The GitHub connector now only handles Git hygiene checks (branches, PRs, commits) which DataSight does not provide.
✓ Teambook is the source for team/pod details. Team ID, team lead, pod structure — read from Teambook API. This pre-populates the team section of the App Registry, reducing the manual burden on app owners.
✓ Scoring architecture is now extensible by design. New sub-metrics (CR auto-creation, release page creation, future automation items) can be added to any pillar without changing the scoring engine. Each sub-metric is a named, weighted entry in a config file — add a line, get a score.
⚠ Legacy/vendor/iSeries apps will have structurally low RF. The solution is weighted portfolio targets — not uniform 280/70 = 4/week for every app. Modern apps compensate for legacy apps in the portfolio average. Full model in Section 4.

Section 02
Extensible Scoring Architecture

The scoring engine reads sub-metrics from a config file — not from hardcoded logic. Adding a new automation item (CR auto-creation, release page creation, future items) means adding one line to a YAML config. No code change. No engine rebuild. This is how the platform evolves.

How it works: Each pillar has a scoring_config.yaml that lists sub-metrics with a weight and source. The engine loops through whatever is in the config. You add a new metric line, set its weight, set its source, and it appears in the next scoring run — on the leaderboard, in team alerts, and in the detailed breakdown.

Section 03
Legacy Apps, Mixed Stacks & the RF Portfolio Model

Not every app can hit the same RF target. A vendor-managed iSeries app releasing quarterly is never hitting 4 releases/week — and shouldn't be held to that. Your job as DevOps Champion is a portfolio average of 280. Here's how to model it fairly and how to discuss it with each app owner.

The key insight: You don't need every app to hit 4 releases/week. You need the portfolio average to reach 280/month. That means modern apps need to release MORE to compensate for legacy apps that structurally cannot. This is a portfolio optimisation problem, not a uniform target problem.

How to explain this to individual app owners: "Your app's velocity score is measured against a target set for your stack type — not against every other app. A legacy app hitting its 1.5/month target gets full velocity score. A modern app missing its 10/month target loses score. The portfolio average is my responsibility. Your responsibility is hitting your stack-appropriate target."
For vendor/iSeries apps with no RF: Their DPI score is calculated on the remaining 4 pillars only, re-weighted to sum to 100. Their RF is excluded from the portfolio total. They still appear on the leaderboard and can improve through Flow, Stability, Automation, and AI pillars — giving the team an active path to improvement despite the RF constraint.
Section 04
App Registry — Intake Plan

Getting 70 apps registered is the #1 critical path item. Here's how to do it in 2 weeks with 3–4 people, using a lightweight Google Form or Jira intake process that feeds directly into the YAML registry.

Step-by-Step Intake Process

1
Pre-populate from Teambook + DataSight
Pull team/pod data from Teambook (team ID, team lead name, email). Pull app names and existing RF/LTDD data from DataSight. Create one pre-filled YAML stub per app — 70 files with what you already know. App owners only fill in what's missing.
Owner: Person 1 · Time: 2 days
2
Send a short Google Form (not a YAML) to each app owner
Do NOT ask app owners to edit YAML files. Create a Google Form (or Jira issue per app) with exactly 12 questions — the minimum needed to compute a baseline DPI score. Everything else has defaults. Champion fills the form with them during the 1:1 meeting you're already having.
Owner: Champion + Person 2 · Time: During app owner meetings (next 2 days)
3
Person 3 converts form responses to YAML files in real-time
As each meeting ends and the form is submitted, Person 3 has a script that converts the Google Form CSV export directly into app YAML files. PR raised, you approve, it's in the registry within 24 hours of each meeting.
Owner: Person 3 · Time: Parallel to meetings
4
Person 4 runs first baseline DPI score against whatever is registered
Don't wait for all 70 apps. After 20 apps are in, run the first score. Publish a partial leaderboard. This creates urgency for remaining app owners to submit their data — they don't want to be missing from the leaderboard.
Owner: Person 4 · Time: End of Week 1
5
Registry completeness as a DPI metric itself
Teams with incomplete registry entries get a visible "Registry Incomplete ⚠️" flag on the leaderboard and their score is shown as "N/A" rather than 0. This is more motivating than a zero — people hate N/A more than a low score.
Owner: Champion · Time: At first leaderboard publish

Section 07 · For Your Next 2 Days
App Owner Interview Guide

Use this in every 1:1 over the next 2 days. Each meeting should be 30–45 minutes. The goal: understand the individual levers, classify the app, fill the 12 registry questions, and leave each owner with a personal improvement target they believe in. Person 3 sits in every meeting and fills the form in real-time.

Meeting setup: Don't send this as a form in advance. Do it live in the meeting. Pre-populate what you already know from DataSight (their current RF, LTDD). Open with their data — it shows you've done your homework and immediately makes the conversation concrete.
Opening 5 min
Set the tone. This is not a performance review. It's a problem-solving session. You own the portfolio target. They own their app's levers.
  • "Your app is currently at [X] releases/month and [Y] days LTDD from DataSight. Your stack is [Z]. Before we talk targets, I want to understand your world — what does a release actually look like for your team today, end to end?"
    Opens them up. You're asking about process, not judging the number. Gets them talking about reality, not aspiration.
  • "Walk me through the last release you did — from the point a developer merged their code to the point it was in production. What happened, who was involved, how long did it take?"
    KEY This one question will reveal more blockers than any checklist. Listen for: manual steps, waiting time, who needs to approve, what can fail.
Blocker Discovery 10 min
Find the actual constraint — not the assumed one. Most teams know what's slowing them down. They just haven't been asked in a structured way.
  • "If I could remove one thing from your release process tomorrow, what would make the biggest difference to how often you can release?"
    RF Lever The single most important question. Write down the exact words they use — this becomes their personal improvement target.
  • "How many people need to approve a release before it goes to production? Are any of those approvals automated or are they all manual sign-offs?"
    Registry Populates approval_gate_count. Also surfaces culture blockers (fear of releasing) vs. process blockers.
  • "Does someone with privileged or elevated access need to be involved in the actual deployment step? Could you deploy without them if you needed to?"
    Registry KEY Populates priv_access_for_deploy. This is often the single biggest blocker to zero-touch — many teams don't realise it until you ask directly.
  • "How big are your typical code changes when you release? Are you releasing lots of small changes or batching up big ones? Why?"
    RF Lever Reveals batching behaviour. If they're batching, it's usually because releasing feels risky or expensive. That's the real problem to solve.
  • "What happens after a failed deployment? How long does it take to roll back or fix forward, and who's involved?"
    Surfaces MTTR reality and whether rollback is automated. Teams with painful rollbacks release less often because failure is expensive.
Pipeline & Stack Reality 8 min
Understand what they have, what's automated, and whether the standard pipeline applies to them. Critical for the registry Y/N flags.
  • "Does your CI build trigger automatically when a developer pushes code? Or does someone need to manually kick it off?"
    Registry ci_automated Y/N. Sounds basic — surprising how many teams still have manual CI triggers.
  • "After CI passes, does deployment happen automatically, or does someone manually push a button to deploy?"
    Registry cd_automated Y/N. Even "we have a pipeline" teams often have a manual trigger for the actual deploy step.
  • "Are you on the org standard pipeline template? If not, what's different about your pipeline and what would need to change to adopt the standard?"
    Registry KEY Standard pipeline adoption is a quick win that unlocks a score improvement AND forces the conversation about what's blocking adoption.
  • "What's your tech stack — is it primarily [Java/Spring / .NET / Node / Python / iSeries / vendor-managed / something else]? Are there multiple stacks across your repos?"
    Registry Populates stack field. Also helps you set the right RF target tier for this app in the portfolio model.
  • "For your legacy or vendor-managed components — what does a change to those look like? Who controls the release schedule?"
    Critical for legacy apps. If a vendor controls releases, RF is not their lever. Redirect to LTDD and other pillars instead.
Setting Personal Targets 8 min
This is the most important part. You want them to leave with a target they believe is achievable and three concrete actions that will get them there. Don't impose the target — co-create it.
  • "Based on what you've told me — your stack, your current RF, your blockers — I'm thinking a realistic target for your app is [X] releases/month in 3 months. Does that feel right, too low, or impossible?"
    KEY Let them react. If they say impossible — that's the conversation. If they say "too easy" — push them higher. The target they co-create will be owned, not resisted.
  • "If you hit [X] releases/month by Month 3, what would need to be true about your pipeline that isn't true today? Let's list the 3 most important changes."
    RF Lever Works backwards from the target. Makes the path concrete. These 3 things become their personal action plan tracked in the DPI score.
  • "What would make you hesitate to release more often, even if the pipeline was perfect? Is there a cultural or political reason releases are batched up?"
    Surfaces fear of releasing — often the real constraint behind every technical blocker. If the answer is "my manager wants a big release for visibility" — that's a leadership conversation you now know you need to have.
  • "What support do you need from me — as DevOps Champion — that would make the biggest difference in the next 4 weeks?"
    Ends the conversation in a collaborative mode. Shows you're here to unblock, not to inspect. Answers to this question are your priority action list after the meetings.
AI & Automation Adoption 5 min
Quick pulse check on where they are with AI tooling. Not a hard ask — a curiosity question. Sets the stage for the AI & Adoption pillar.
  • "Is anyone on your team using AI coding tools — Copilot, Cursor, any AI agent — in their day-to-day development work? Officially or informally?"
    Registry Populates copilot_enabled / ai_tools_declared. "Informally" answers reveal shadow AI adoption you can help formalise.
  • "If I could get you access to an AI coding agent for your whole team tomorrow, what's the first thing you'd use it for — writing tests, code review, documentation, something else?"
    Forward-looking. Shows AI is an offer, not a mandate. Their answer tells you how mature their AI thinking is and what training/enablement they'd need.
Closing & Registry Confirmation 4 min
Confirm the registry data is captured, set expectations for what happens next, and end on a clear commitment from both sides.
  • "[Person 3] has captured your app details and will create your DPI profile in the registry. You'll receive it for review by [date]. The first leaderboard goes live [Week 2]. Any questions about how you'll be scored?"
    Sets clear expectations. Removes the fear of the unknown score. They know when they'll see it and can ask questions now rather than complain later.
  • "One last thing: who is your Release Champion — the person on your team who will be the go-to for hitting your RF target? It doesn't have to be you."
    Registry Populates release_champion. Distributing accountability below the team lead level is critical at scale. This is the person who'll receive the weekly hygiene alerts.
After the meeting — what to do with the answers:
1. Person 3 converts form response to YAML within 4 hours.
2. You review the "biggest blocker" answers across all 70 apps — patterns will emerge (e.g. "30 apps say privileged access is their #1 blocker"). That pattern becomes your next mandate.
3. Stack classification drives the RF target per app in the portfolio model.
4. Apps where "vendor controls releases" are flagged as RF-excluded in the registry (their DPI is computed on 4 pillars, not 5).
5. The 3 personal improvement actions per app become the "recommended actions" shown on each team's DPI score card.
